    Co-creation sign system:

    A case study of the Fukuoka Botanical

    To create a more interesting signage system for the Fukuoka Botanical, achieving a more efficient and engaging narrative design

    より面白い植物園のサインシステムを創造し、より効率的で魅力的なナラティブデザインを実現します

    Garden tourism

    Every year, more than 200 million people visit botanical gardens around the world, which is about one in every thirty-three people globally.

    Therefore, botanical gardens are not only considered excellent places for recreation, but also play important roles in education, conservation of plants and natural resources, and scientific research

    Background

    Today, the number of foreign tourists visiting Japan is increasing rapidly. In response, both national and local governments have developed guidelines for improving information signage and are revising existing signs.

    Fukuoka City also lists "actively incorporating foreign languages and pictograms into signage to create an image suitable for an international city" as one of its goals in developing urban signage.

    Purpose

    Some believe that without explanation, a botanical garden is nothing more than an attractive park.

    A botanical garden is a place for conveying specific narratives about place, belonging, and the relationship between humans and the environment.

    Therefore, the purpose of this study is to explore how to enhance the information delivery efficiency of narrative signage in botanical gardens through co-creative design.

    Significance

    The significance of this research lies in enhancing the inclusiveness, narrative quality, and user engagement of botanical garden signage systems through co-creation design methods.

    OWhile traditional signage focuses mainly on accessibility and information delivery, this study emphasizes how design can strengthen emotional connections between people, nature, and space.

    Passive problem

    "Biology is the great adventure of our time; let us not kill it with passive, abstract writing."

    Traditional interpretive signage often follows scientific writing conventions, frequently using passive voice. However, overusing passive voice can lead to ineffective and boring prose.

    In botanical gardens, interpretive signage should not blindly adhere to such rules—instead, it should embrace subjectivity, focusing on clarifying meaning and enhancing readability.

    Information overload

    Whether it's interpretive signage or information panels, once the number and density of interpretive elements exceed a certain threshold, it can lead to information overload and/or visual pollution, which negatively impacts visitors.

    Visitors tend to absorb only the information they are interested in, a phenomenon psychologists call “selective attention.”

    Therefore, tourism elements rich in local imagery are more likely to captivate and emotionally move visitors.

    -To shed light on the role of signage as a tool for social inclusion and help build a more equitable signage system that reflects cultural diversity.

    PARTICIPATORY DESIGN:

    Using Graffiti as a Model

    Inspired by "graffiti," this research will be carried out through workshops under the strategy of participatory design

    「グラフィティ」をインスピレーションとして、参加型デザインの戦略の下でワークショップを通じて本研究を完成させる

    Workshop

    Through workshops, co-create prototypes of the JSL signage system with disabled artists, using "graffiti" as the key concept. Test the prototypes for functional and symbolic communication efficiency, collect datas, and continuously refine them.

    "graffiti"

    One of the earliest functions of graffiti was its use by minority language speakers in bilingual countries to protest and demand the inclusion of minority languages in public signage systems.

    At the same time, graffiti also carries a strong humanistic attribute and has a low learning threshold, making it quickly usable by participants of any artistic level in workshops.

    disabled artists

    The workshop will not be limited to sign language users or individuals with hearing impairments, but will invite artists from all disability categories.

    impairments are transitioning from being seen as part of the "disability group" to becoming "sign language users" in order to overcome discrimination. Therefore, in the workshop, all disability groups will be treated as "special language users."

    datas

    The data I aim to collect in this workshop are as follows:

    Quantitative data: The impact of the JSL signage system on the wayfinding efficiency of different groups.

    Qualitative data: The opinions of different groups on the cultural connotations presented by the JSL signage system.
